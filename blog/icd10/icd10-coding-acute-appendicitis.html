ICD-10 Coding for Acute Appendicitis: K35 Series Complete Guide

By Medical Coding Team • Updated January 2026 • 8 min read

Acute appendicitis remains one of the most common surgical emergencies, with over 300,000 appendectomies performed annually in the United States. For surgeons, residents, physician assistants, and medical coders, accurate ICD-10 coding of appendicitis is critical for proper reimbursement, quality metrics, and clinical documentation. The K35 series provides specific codes that capture the complexity and severity of appendiceal pathology.

This comprehensive guide covers the essential K35 codes used in appendicitis cases, focusing on the most clinically relevant distinctions that impact coding decisions and reimbursement outcomes.

Understanding the K35 Code Series Structure

The ICD-10-CM K35 category covers acute appendicitis and is structured to capture key clinical distinctions that affect patient management and surgical approach. The primary differentiating factors include:

  • Perforation status: Non-perforated vs. perforated appendicitis
  • Abscess formation: Presence or absence of abscess
  • Peritonitis: Localized vs. generalized peritoneal involvement
  • Other complications: Such as gangrene or other specified conditions
Clinical Pearl: The distinction between perforated and non-perforated appendicitis is crucial not only for coding but also for determining appropriate surgical approach, antibiotic therapy duration, and expected length of stay.

Primary K35 Codes for Acute Appendicitis

K35.2
Acute appendicitis with generalized peritonitis
Use when perforation has resulted in widespread peritoneal contamination

K35.2 represents the most severe form of acute appendicitis where perforation has led to generalized peritoneal contamination. This diagnosis typically requires more aggressive surgical intervention, prolonged antibiotic therapy, and extended hospitalization. Documentation should clearly indicate evidence of generalized peritonitis such as diffuse abdominal tenderness, guarding, or imaging findings consistent with widespread peritoneal fluid or inflammation.

K35.3
Acute appendicitis with localized peritonitis
Perforation with localized inflammatory response, may include phlegmon formation

K35.3 captures cases where appendiceal perforation has occurred but the peritoneal response remains localized. This often presents with formation of a phlegmon or contained perforation. While still requiring urgent surgical intervention, these cases typically have better outcomes than those with generalized peritonitis. Clinical documentation should specify the localized nature of the inflammatory process.

K35.80
Unspecified acute appendicitis
Use when specific details about perforation or complications are not documented

K35.80 serves as the default code when the clinical documentation does not provide sufficient detail to assign a more specific code. While less precise, this code is appropriate when the operative report or clinical notes simply document "acute appendicitis" without detailing complications or anatomical findings.

K35.89
Other acute appendicitis
For appendicitis with complications not elsewhere classified

K35.89 captures acute appendicitis with complications or features that don't fit into the other specific categories. This might include cases with unusual presentations, specific complications like appendiceal neoplasm with acute inflammation, or other documented features that require this broader categorization.

Critical Documentation Elements

Accurate coding depends heavily on thorough clinical documentation. Surgeons and residents should ensure their operative reports and clinical notes include:

Perforation Status

  • Clear statement of whether perforation is present
  • Size and location of perforation if identified
  • Description of peritoneal contamination
  • Presence of free fluid or purulent material

Abscess Formation

  • Location and size of any identified abscesses
  • Whether abscess is contained or communicating
  • Relationship to adjacent structures
  • Method of drainage if performed

Peritonitis Extent

  • Localized vs. generalized peritoneal involvement
  • Anatomical extent of contamination
  • Presence of fibrinous exudate
  • Evidence of systemic inflammatory response
Clinical Finding Coding Impact Documentation Requirement
Visible perforation at surgery Directs to K35.2 or K35.3 Size, location, contamination extent
Free purulent fluid Suggests generalized peritonitis Quantity, distribution, culture results

Common Coding Scenarios and Solutions

Scenario 1: Laparoscopic Appendectomy with Perforation

A 34-year-old patient undergoes laparoscopic appendectomy. The operative report states: "Perforated appendicitis with localized abscess formation in the right lower quadrant. Minimal free fluid. Appendix removed intact after drainage of localized collection."

Appropriate Code: K35.3 (Acute appendicitis with localized peritonitis)

Rationale: The perforation with localized abscess but minimal free fluid indicates localized rather than generalized peritonitis.

Scenario 2: Open Appendectomy with Extensive Contamination

A 67-year-old patient presents with severe sepsis. Operative findings: "Extensively perforated appendicitis with large volume purulent ascites throughout all four abdominal quadrants. Multiple areas of fibrinous exudate on bowel surfaces."

Appropriate Code: K35.2 (Acute appendicitis with generalized peritonitis)

Rationale: The widespread purulent ascites and diffuse fibrinous exudate clearly indicate generalized peritonitis.

Scenario 3: Simple Acute Appendicitis

A 28-year-old patient undergoes appendectomy. Operative note: "Acutely inflamed appendix, no perforation identified, minimal inflammatory response, appendix removed without complication."

Appropriate Code: K35.9 (Acute appendicitis, unspecified)

Rationale: Simple acute appendicitis without complications fits this unspecified category.

Coding Pitfalls to Avoid

Over-coding Severity

Avoid automatically assigning codes for perforated appendicitis based solely on clinical presentation. The diagnosis must be confirmed surgically and clearly documented in the operative report.

Under-documenting Complexity

Surgeons should resist the urge to oversimplify operative findings. Detailed documentation of anatomical findings, contamination extent, and surgical complexity supports appropriate code assignment and reflects the true clinical complexity.

Ignoring Pathology Correlation

When operative findings are ambiguous, pathology reports provide crucial confirmation of acute inflammation and can support or refute the clinical impression of acute appendicitis.

Coding Reminder: Always code to the highest level of specificity supported by the documentation. When in doubt, query the surgeon for clarification rather than defaulting to unspecified codes.

Impact on Reimbursement and Quality Metrics

Accurate appendicitis coding affects multiple aspects of healthcare delivery:

  • DRG Assignment: Perforated appendicitis typically results in higher-weighted DRGs
  • Length of Stay Expectations: More severe codes allow for longer expected stays
  • Quality Indicators: Proper coding supports appropriate risk adjustment
  • Resource Utilization: Reflects true complexity of care provided

Integration with CPT Coding

ICD-10 diagnosis codes work in tandem with CPT procedure codes. Common appendectomy CPT codes include:

  • 44970 - Laparoscopic appendectomy
  • 44960 - Open appendectomy
  • 44979 - Unlisted laparoscopic appendix procedure

The complexity documented in your ICD-10 coding should align with the surgical approach and complexity reflected in your CPT code selection.

Best Practices for Clinical Documentation

To ensure optimal coding accuracy, adopt these documentation practices:

  1. Use specific anatomical descriptions: Describe location, size, and extent of pathology
  2. Document decision-making rationale: Explain why specific surgical approaches were chosen
  3. Include photographic documentation: When available, surgical photos support coding decisions
  4. Correlate with imaging findings: Reference pre-operative CT or ultrasound findings
  5. Note unexpected findings: Document any surprises that affected surgical complexity

Conclusion

Mastering ICD-10 coding for acute appendicitis requires understanding the clinical distinctions that drive code selection within the K35 series. The key differentiators—perforation status, peritonitis extent, and abscess formation—must be clearly documented to support appropriate code assignment.

Accurate coding serves multiple stakeholders: it ensures appropriate reimbursement for hospitals, supports quality reporting initiatives, and provides accurate data for research and population health management. By maintaining high standards for clinical documentation and understanding the nuances of the K35 code series, surgical teams can ensure their coding practices reflect the true complexity and quality of care they provide.

Remember that coding is not just an administrative task—it's a critical component of the medical record that tells the story of patient care. When documentation accurately reflects clinical reality, coding accuracy naturally follows, benefiting patients, providers, and the healthcare system as a whole.
